3) Geographic Metadata Assignment

Each document chunk in the corpus is associated with geographic metadata derived from its source. We employ a three-tier assignment strategy based on the jurisdictional scope of the document:

County-level documents. Publications originating from a specific county Extension office (e.g., Robeson County crop reports, Johnston County pest alerts) are assigned the geographic centroid of the corresponding county. For example, a pest advisory from the Robeson County Extension office is mapped to coordinates (34.63°N, 79.10°W), the centroid of Robeson County, North Carolina.

State-level documents. Publications with statewide applicability, such as the NC Soybean Production Guide (AG-835) or the NC Organic Commodities Production Guide (AG-660), are assigned the centroid of North Carolina (35.78°N, 79.63°W). These documents represent authoritative state-level guidance applicable across the entire state but with particular relevance to producers within North Carolina.

National and international documents. Peer-reviewed journal articles from Scopus, textbook excerpts, and other documents without a specific geographic jurisdiction are assigned a null spatial attribute. During retrieval, these documents are ranked purely by semantic similarity (i.e., their spatial score component is omitted from Equation 2), ensuring they remain accessible but do not receive geographic prioritization.

The geodesic distance d(guser, gdoc) in Equation (1) is computed using the Haversine formula:

d = 2R · arcsin(√(sin²(Δφ/2) + cos(φ₁)·cos(φ₂)·sin²(Δλ/2)))

where R = 6,371 km is the Earth's radius, φ represents latitude, and λ represents longitude. The distance is normalized by a maximum regional threshold Dmax = 500 km, representing the approximate radius of the Southeast agricultural region. This normalization ensures that all documents within North Carolina receive Sdistance values in the range [0.67, 1.0], while documents from adjacent states (e.g., Virginia, South Carolina) receive moderately reduced scores, and documents from distant regions receive scores approaching 0.5.

In our corpus, this assignment yields 2,946 county-level chunks (from 17 county Extension offices), 1,584 state-level chunks (from statewide production guides and crop portal publications), and approximately 65,000 national/international chunks (from Scopus papers and textbooks) with null spatial attributes.

F. Ablation Study

To isolate the contribution of the spatial retrieval component, we conduct a systematic ablation study with four conditions: (1) the full AgriRegion system with spatial-semantic retrieval (α = 0.5), (2) semantic-only RAG with the spatial component disabled (α = 0), (3) reduced retrieval depth (Top-k = 2), and (4) retrieval with random documents. All conditions use the same corpus, embedding model, and generation model, differing only in the retrieval configuration.

The most critical comparison is between the full system (α = 0.5) and semantic-only RAG (α = 0). Disabling the spatial component results in a decrease of 0.06 F1 (0.82 → 0.76) and 0.07 RAGA-Precision (0.86 → 0.79). This demonstrates that the geospatial re-ranking provides measurable improvement beyond standard semantic retrieval alone.

The improvement is not uniform across domains. Spatial retrieval provides the largest gains in subfields requiring location-specific recommendations: Irrigation (+0.09 F1 over α=0), Soil Science (+0.07 F1), and Plant Pathology (+0.06 F1). These domains frequently involve threshold-based, rate-specific, or conditional agronomic recommendations that vary by region—precisely the scenarios where local Extension documents provide critical context that generic retrieval may miss.

Comparing semantic RAG (α=0) to no retrieval confirms that RAG itself provides substantial benefit (+0.09 F1). However, the additional gain from spatial awareness (+0.06 F1) demonstrates that the geospatial component is not merely decorative—it meaningfully improves retrieval quality by prioritizing locally relevant evidence.

A. Limitations

Several limitations of the current work should be acknowledged. First, the evaluation is restricted to North Carolina, a single US state. While NC represents a diverse agricultural landscape—encompassing Coastal Plain, Piedmont, and Mountain regions with distinct soil types, climates, and cropping systems—the generalizability of the spatial retrieval approach to other states or countries has not been empirically validated. We note, however, that the framework is designed to be modular: any state's Cooperative Extension system can be integrated by replacing the document corpus and updating the county centroid coordinates.

Second, the AgriRegion-Eval benchmark contains 160 questions authored by domain experts. While this provides focused evaluation across 12 agricultural subfields, a larger evaluation set would strengthen statistical conclusions. To address this, we have developed NCBench, a companion dataset of 2,869 QA pairs derived from NC Extension publications, which can serve as an expanded evaluation resource in future work.

Third, the current system assigns geographic metadata at the county or state level using centroid coordinates. This simplification does not capture within-county variation (e.g., soil type differences between eastern and western portions of a large county) or ecological zones that cross county boundaries. Future work could incorporate finer-grained geospatial features such as soil survey data or USDA Plant Hardiness Zones.

Fourth, the RAGAS evaluation metrics rely on embedding-based similarity and LLM-based assessment, which may not perfectly correlate with human expert judgment. We have conducted a preliminary human evaluation on a 50-question subset (see Section IV.H) to validate automated scores, but a larger-scale human evaluation remains future work.

Data Availability

The AgriRegion-Eval benchmark dataset (160 questions with reference answers across 12 agricultural subfields), the NCBench companion dataset (2,869 QA pairs from NC Extension publications), all evaluation scripts, and the document processing pipeline are publicly available at: https://github.com/mdfanuel-ux/ncbench

An interactive demonstration of the dataset and benchmark results is available at: https://mdfanuel-ux.github.io/ncbench/

The agricultural corpus used for retrieval is constructed from publicly available NC Cooperative Extension publications, Scopus-indexed journal articles, and standard agricultural textbooks as described in Section IV.A.

NEW: Related Work Additions

Add these paragraphs to Section II (Related Work)

Add to Section II.B (after AgriGPT discussion):

Several other systems have explored RAG for agricultural applications. Agri-Query [R1] proposes a retrieval-based QA system for agricultural extension, though without explicit geospatial constraints. ShizishanGPT [R2] develops a Chinese agricultural LLM with retrieval capabilities focused on crop disease diagnosis. AgriHubi [R3] presents a multi-agent agricultural advisory system combining retrieval with specialized sub-agents. While these systems demonstrate the value of retrieval augmentation in agriculture, none explicitly incorporate geospatial metadata into the retrieval pipeline or enforce regional constraints during re-ranking—the core contribution of AgriRegion.

In the broader NLP literature, Spatial-RAG [R4] addresses spatial reasoning in retrieval-augmented generation for general geographic questions. Our work differs in three key aspects: (1) we target a specific professional domain where spatial context directly affects recommendation validity, (2) we use verified institutional documents (Cooperative Extension services) rather than web-scraped content, and (3) our distance decay function is calibrated for agricultural relevance zones (county → state → region) rather than arbitrary geographic queries.
