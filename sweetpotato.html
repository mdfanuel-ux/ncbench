🍠 NC Sweetpotato

A deep-dive into North Carolina sweetpotato production — part of the NCBench project

Overview

North Carolina is the #1 sweetpotato producer in the United States, accounting for over 60% of national production. With over 400 growers and approximately 95,000 harvested acres, sweetpotato is NC's top vegetable crop and the official state vegetable.

Sweetpotatoes are grown primarily in the Coastal Plain of eastern North Carolina, where the hot, moist climate and rich, fertile sandy loam soils create ideal growing conditions. They are planted in May or June from cuttings called slips and harvested in late August through October. Unlike soybean, which has a comprehensive 12-chapter production guide, sweetpotato knowledge in NC Extension is scattered across multiple sources — making it a prime candidate for NCBench's consolidation effort.

History & Significance

Sweetpotatoes have been cultivated in the Americas for over 5,000 years, originating in Central and South America. Spanish explorers brought them to Europe and Asia in the 1500s. In North Carolina, sweetpotato production has been commercially significant since the early 1900s.

NC has maintained its #1 ranking since 1971 — over 50 consecutive years as the nation's top producer. The state's dominance grew with the development of improved varieties at NC State University, particularly 'Covington' (released 2005), which revolutionized the industry with its consistent shape, disease resistance, and superior eating quality.

Sweetpotato is the official state vegetable of North Carolina (designated 1995). Wilson County alone produces more sweetpotatoes than the entire state of Louisiana, the fourth-highest producing state.

Sweetpotato Production

🌱
Slips & Planting

Grown from vine cuttings (slips), not seeds. Planted May–June in well-drained sandy loam with full sunlight. Disease-free certified slips are critical.

🌡️
Curing

After harvest, roots are cured at 85°F and 85–90% humidity for 4–7 days. This heals wounds, converts starch to sugar, and extends storage life to 6–12 months.

📦
Storage

Stored at 55–60°F after curing. Proper storage is critical — sweetpotatoes are sensitive to chilling injury below 50°F and decay above 65°F.

🏪
Markets

Fresh market, processing (canned, frozen, baby food), and export. NC supplies major grocery chains and food processors year-round from storage.

Key Varieties

Covington

Developed by NC State. 80%+ of NC production. Consistent yield, disease resistance, excellent eating quality. The industry standard.

Beauregard

Popular for early-season harvest. Good yield but more susceptible to some diseases than Covington.

Orleans & Bayou Belle

Newer varieties with improved resistance to soil-borne diseases. Promising for organic systems and NC sandy loam soils.

Curing & Storage — The Critical Difference

Unlike most crops, sweetpotatoes require a post-harvest curing process that fundamentally changes the product. Proper curing is what transforms a starchy root into the sweet, flavorful vegetable consumers expect. This is one of the most knowledge-intensive aspects of sweetpotato production — and a key area where NCBench QA pairs will be valuable.

Why This Matters for NCBench

Curing and storage knowledge is highly specialized and region-specific. Temperature tolerances, humidity control, facility design, and timing decisions vary by variety and climate. This is exactly the type of expert knowledge that LLMs currently lack — and that NCBench aims to capture through QA pairs generated from Extension documents on post-harvest handling.

Production Challenges

Sweetpotato cultivation involves unique challenges not shared by grain crops like soybean. These are the key areas where NC Extension provides guidance — and where NCBench QA pairs will be most valuable.

🦠 Diseases

Fusarium wilt, black rot, scurf, Streptomyces soil rot, and storage rots. Disease-free slips and field rotation are primary controls.

Plant Pathology portal →

🐛 Insect Pests

Wireworms, flea beetles, white grubs, and sweetpotato weevil (in southern counties). Soil insecticides and cultural controls.

🌡️ Curing & Storage

Improper curing leads to decay and poor flavor. Temperature and humidity control is critical. Many small growers lack proper curing facilities.

Harvesting & curing guide →

🧪 Food Safety

Post-harvest handling, washing, packing house sanitation. NC Fresh Produce Safety provides commodity-specific guidance.

Food safety brochures →

🌿 Weed Management

Limited herbicide options compared to soybean. Mechanical cultivation and hand weeding still common. Critical during vine establishment.

📊 No Comprehensive Guide

Unlike soybean (12-chapter guide), sweetpotato knowledge is scattered across factsheets, pathology portals, and organic production chapters. NCBench aims to consolidate this.
