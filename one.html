Overview & Motivation

NCBench is a large-scale instruction tuning dataset for agricultural language models, targeting soybean and sweetpotato farming in the United States. Built on the methodology of the CROP dataset — which produced 68,210 QA pairs across rice and corn — NCBench extends the same proven pipeline to crops relevant to North Carolina and the broader US agricultural landscape.

The CROP dataset demonstrated that structured, domain-specific QA data significantly improves agricultural reasoning in LLMs. Its construction pipeline — prompt-based generation, task decomposition, and multi-turn dialogue synthesis — is largely crop-agnostic, meaning it transfers directly to new domains with minimal adaptation. NCBench leverages this reusability: the same templates, taxonomy, and quality controls apply to soybean and sweetpotato with only the source documents and regional context changing.

NCBench: What We're Building

While the CROP dataset covers rice and corn with a focus on Chinese agriculture, no equivalent instruction tuning dataset exists for US-grown crops. NCBench fills this gap by targeting soybean and sweetpotato — two crops with significant economic importance to North Carolina and the broader US agricultural landscape.

Why These Crops

🫘
Soybean

Soybean is one of the most widely grown crops in the US, with North Carolina ranking among the top producing states in the Southeast. It is critical for animal feed, oil production, and soil nitrogen fixation in crop rotation systems. Despite its importance, LLMs have limited domain-specific knowledge about US soybean agronomy, pest management, and regional growing practices.

🍠
Sweetpotato

North Carolina is the #1 sweetpotato producer in the United States, accounting for over half of national production. Sweetpotato cultivation involves unique challenges — curing, storage, pest pressure from wireworms and flea beetles, and variety selection for different markets. This specialized knowledge is poorly represented in current LLM training data.

Multi-Turn Dialogue Example

In the multi-turn pipeline, an LLM creates tasks under domain expert guidance and assigns roles to two agents. Using task-dependent prompts and RAG, the LLM generates realistic consultations. Below is an actual example from the CROP dataset — a problem-solving pest control scenario.

Figure adapted from the CROP paper (Figure 24).

🧑‍🌾
AI User:
Farmer
Q1: I've been noticing some of my rice plants are stunted and the leaves are turning yellow. What could be causing this issue?
👨‍🔬
AI Assistant:
Crop Pestologist
A1: Yellowing and stunting in rice can be symptoms of several issues including nutrient deficiencies, water stress, or pest infestations such as stem borers or leaf folders. Have you noticed any visible insects on the plants or any other unusual signs?
Q2: Yes, I've seen some moths around the plants in the evening and there are holes in the leaves. Could this be related to the yellowing?
A2: It sounds like your rice crop might be infested with stem borers or leaf folders. These pests can cause the symptoms you're describing as they damage the plant's ability to transport nutrients and water by boring into the stems or folding the leaves.
Q3: What can I do to manage these pests and protect my crop?
A3: Considering the infestation, it would be wise to apply integrated pest management practices. This includes using pheromone traps to monitor and reduce moth populations and possibly applying biological control agents like Trichogramma wasps, which are natural enemies of stem borers. Also, ensure that your crop is healthy with proper nutrition and water management to resist pest damage more effectively.
Q4: Thank you for the advice. I'll look into these options. Is there a specific time or method for applying these biological controls?
A4: For biological controls like Trichogramma wasps, it's best to release them early in the season before the pest population peaks. Local agricultural extension services or a professional pest management expert can provide guidance on timing and application methods specific to your area to ensure the best results.

Problem-solving scenario: Pest Control — from the CROP multi-turn dialogue dataset (Figure 24).

Methodology Adaptation (Why This Is Feasible)

This project builds directly on the methodology introduced in the CROP dataset. The pipeline — prompt-based QA generation, task decomposition, multi-turn dialogue synthesis, and LLM-based filtering — is crop-agnostic and transfers directly to new domains. Our prior experience with the CROP dataset (rice + corn) means the task taxonomy, generation framework, and grading strategy are already validated and ready to reuse.

CROP → NCBench: What Stays, What Changes

Transfers Directly
  • Task taxonomy (QA, NER, Summary, Event Extraction)
  • Single-turn & multi-turn generation pipelines
  • LLM-based filtering and quality grading
  • Prompt template structure and format
Changes for NCBench
  • Crop domain → soybean & sweetpotato
  • Data sources → US/NC extension docs, English-only
  • Regional grounding → North Carolina / Midwest
  • Literature base → US agricultural practices

Prompt Template — Closed-Book Q&A (English)

Adapted from the CROP paper (Figure 12). This is the actual prompt structure used to generate QA pairs.

Topic Description
Instruct the model to return professional knowledge Q&A. There are no external reference materials provided in the produced question.
System Prompt
Role Description
You are an expert in the field of study relevant to the academic paper enclosed within the <doc></doc> tags. Please generate a set of question-answer pairs that reflect the key findings and discussions in the paper.
Reference Text
<doc> In the recent past, population explosion has augmented urbanization…… </doc>
Guidelines
  1. The number of QA pairs should be determined by the depth and breadth of the paper, with a minimum of N_min and a maximum of N_max.
  2. Questions should cover various aspects of the paper, avoid focusing solely on numerical data, and refrain from yes/no questions or questions with obvious answers.
  3. Ensure QA pairs cover: Stress Resistance, Pests and Diseases, Cultivation Techniques, Breeding, Characteristics, Suitable Planting Area, etc.
  4. Ensure that the answers follow a logical sequence and are supported by information in the paper.
  5. You may refer to the example provided within the <example> tags, but ensure the output strictly follows the format.
Example Dialogue
Question: How to achieve a high yield in the cultivation of Y302 through fertilizer management?
Answer: In the cultivation of Y302, high yield is achieved through the scientific ratio and timely application of base fertilizer, topdressing, ear fertilizer, and grain fertilizer. Considering the longer growth time and high yield potential of Y302, organic fertilizer needs to be added.
📤 Example LLM Output
Q1: What was the primary objective of the field research conducted on fourteen early genotypes of maize?
A1: The primary objective was to explore the roles of chlorophyll content and its relation with other yield attributing traits in maize.

Q2: What were the specific findings regarding aflatoxin levels in maize using different drying methods?
A2: The research found significant differences in aflatoxin levels among the three drying methods tested. Kitchen drying resulted in the lowest aflatoxin contamination (1.4 ppb), followed by sun drying (1.6 ppb) and dry shelter drying (2.2 ppb).

This template is not crop-specific — it applies to soybean and sweetpotato with no structural changes.

Because the pipeline is already validated, there is no need to redesign the methodology. The focus shifts entirely to data collection and coverage for the new crop domains. Templates, task structures, and quality controls are reused as-is — making this project an extension of a proven framework rather than a build from scratch.

NCBench Preview — Sample QA Pairs

Below are representative examples of the type of QA pairs NCBench will produce. These illustrate the target format, depth, and regional specificity.

🫘 Soybean — Pest Management
Q: What is the most economically damaging pest of soybean in the southeastern United States, and what management strategies are recommended?

A: The soybean cyst nematode (Heterodera glycines) is the most economically damaging pest of soybean in the Southeast. Management strategies include planting resistant varieties, rotating with non-host crops such as corn or cotton, and monitoring soil nematode populations through fall sampling. In North Carolina, the NC Cooperative Extension recommends a minimum 2-year rotation away from soybean in heavily infested fields.
🍠 Sweetpotato — Curing & Storage
Q: Why is curing important for sweetpotatoes after harvest, and what are the recommended conditions?

A: Curing heals harvest wounds, reduces decay during storage, and converts starches to sugars which improves flavor. The recommended conditions are 85°F (29°C) and 85–90% relative humidity for 4–7 days. In North Carolina, curing facilities are typically maintained immediately after harvest in September–October. Proper curing can extend storage life to 6–12 months at 55–60°F.
🫘 Soybean — Planting
Q: What is the optimal planting window for soybean in the North Carolina Piedmont region?

A: The optimal planting window for full-season soybean in the NC Piedmont is mid-April through mid-May. Planting within this window maximizes vegetative growth before the critical reproductive stages coincide with peak summer rainfall. Late planting (after June 15) typically results in 15–25% yield reduction due to shortened growing season and increased drought risk during pod fill.
🍠 Sweetpotato — Variety Selection
Q: What sweetpotato varieties are recommended for commercial production in North Carolina?

A: The primary commercial variety is 'Covington', developed by NC State University, which accounts for over 80% of NC production due to its consistent yield, disease resistance, and excellent eating quality. 'Beauregard' remains popular for early-season harvest. For organic systems, 'Orleans' and 'Bayou Belle' show improved resistance to soil-borne diseases common in NC sandy loam soils.

These are illustrative examples of the target format. Actual NCBench entries will be generated from NC Extension documents and peer-reviewed literature using the CROP pipeline.
