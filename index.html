Overview

The NC Soybean Producers Association (NCSPA) administers checkoff-funded programs for NC soybean growers through research, promotion, and advocacy. The NC Soybean Production Guide, funded by NCSPA and published by NC State Extension, is the authoritative reference for soybean production in the state.

History of Soybeans

Origins & Early Spread

Soybeans originated in Southeast Asia and were first domesticated by Chinese farmers around 1100 BC. By the first century AD, the crop had spread to Japan and across Asia. Soybean seeds first arrived in the Americas in 1765, when a colonist in Georgia planted seed from China. In 1851, seeds were distributed to farmers in Illinois — reportedly from a rescued Japanese fisherman — marking the first steps toward American soybean farming.

Rise in American Agriculture

By the 1870s, soybeans gained popularity as livestock forage, thriving in the hot, humid conditions of states like North Carolina. In 1904, George Washington Carver discovered their value as a source of protein, oil, and soil enrichment — leading to his famous crop rotation system that revitalized cotton farming in the South. In 1919, William Morse co-founded the American Soybean Association and later collected over 10,000 varieties from China to expand research.

Modern Era

Production accelerated during the 1940s when WWII disrupted supply from China, driving demand for soybean-based oils, lubricants, and plastics. Henry Ford explored soy's potential for making plastics and car parts as early as the 1930s. By the 1990s, biotechnology transformed the crop with herbicide-tolerant varieties that increased yields and reduced erosion.

NC Today

Soybeans are grown in all 31 US states, with Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, and Minnesota leading. North Carolina plays a vital role as both a grower and a major consumer due to its large pork and poultry industries. NC farmers grow soybeans alongside corn, wheat, cotton, sweetpotatoes, and peanuts.

Growing Soybeans

🌱
Planting

May–July in NC. Seeds planted ~1.5" deep in rows up to 30" apart. No-till planting into wheat stubble saves time and reduces erosion.

🌿
Growth

Sprouts in 4–7 days. Blooms July–September with small white to violet flowers. Self-pollinating — each flower has male and female parts.

🫘
Maturity

Late September — leaves turn yellow then brown and fall off, exposing mature pods. Ready for harvest by mid-October to November.

🚜
Harvest

Combines cut and separate beans from pods. Transported to grain dealers or on-farm storage. Processed into soybean meal (protein) and soy oil.

Why Soybeans Are Special

🔄 Nitrogen Fixation

Soybeans are legumes that form a symbiotic relationship with bacteria in their root nodules, converting atmospheric nitrogen into plant-usable form. This reduces fertilizer needs and restores nitrogen to the soil for the next crop.

🌾 Rotational Crop

Farmers rotate soybeans with corn, wheat, cotton, and other crops. After soybean harvest, winter wheat can be planted in the nitrogen-enriched soil. Soybeans can also be double-cropped after wheat harvest in the same year.

🧬 Seed Selection

Farmers select seed based on yield potential, drought tolerance, disease resistance, and herbicide tolerance traits (Roundup Ready, Liberty Link, Xtend). NC Official Variety Testing provides local performance data.

Soybeans in North Carolina

NC farmers plant soybeans in all 100 counties from the mountains to the sea, though 75% of production is concentrated in the eastern Coastal Plain. At 1.6 million acres in an average year, soybeans have the biggest footprint of any crop in the state — worth up to $800 million to farmers in a good year.

Soybeans rank in the top 5 commodities by cash receipts in NC. The crop is deeply connected to the state's livestock industry — NC's large pork and poultry operations are major consumers of soybean meal for animal feed. Roughly 95% of NC farms are family-owned and operated.

Soybeans work well in rotation with tobacco, cotton, corn, and wheat — making them an integral part of NC's diverse agricultural landscape. Many farmers double-crop soybeans after winter wheat harvest, maximizing land use within a single growing season.
